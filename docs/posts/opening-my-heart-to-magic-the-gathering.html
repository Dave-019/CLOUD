Opening My Heart To Magic The Gathering

There have been times in life where I’ve said to myself “that thing isn’t for me, and it’s never gonna be for me.”

I’m not sure if it’s a matter of close-mindedness, or stubbornness, or just an effort to trim down the overwhelming infinity of hobby options that life throws our way.

In some cases, I end up being wrong. Given enough time, things change and I change and eventually I decide to give the thing the shot.

I remember thinking Star Trek would never be something for me. Or The Hobbit. I had decided that I was a certain type of nerd, and it wasn’t that type, so I could safely ignore those things. And yet I can say now that I’ve dipped a toe into both and had a good time.

While I hadn’t made a conscious effort to avoid it, I remember thinking that Sailor Moon wasn’t for me. Finally giving it a shot almost twenty years later was one of the best decisions I could’ve made.

I remember thinking that I’d never enjoy exercise or going to the gym. Now, it’s one of the things I look forward to every week.

Some things I still haven’t come around on. I haven’t found a professional sport that I like watching, for instance. Maybe someday one will click, maybe that day will never come. I haven’t dipped into Jojo’s Bizarre Adventure, or Disco Elysium, or plenty of other acclaimed stories.

Lately I’ve been playing a lot of Magic the Gathering. I remember, for years, thinking that was a hobby I was never ever going to touch.

I don’t remember when I first heard about Magic the Gathering.

I assume I must have been a kid. I was online a lot from age nine onwards and this was the kind of thing that is basically in the air in nerdy circles.

My earliest experiences with card games were, not shockingly, Pokemon and Yu-Gi-Oh.

I had a couple of Pokemon cards, but not enough to really play it. I only played it in the stellar Gameboy Color adaptation, a game that features some of my favorite VGM songs of all time.

My brother and I were big fans of watching Yu-Gi-Oh on Saturday mornings, which of course led to us begging our parents to get cards. We got starter decks (I was Kaiba, my older brother was Yugi) and had a blast with it. We eventually started asking our parents for booster packs when we’d see them in the wild. I remember seeing them a lot at card stores (like, greeting card stores…remember those?) and little rinky-dink retail spaces like that.

I was never good at either of these games. I would argue I still haven’t found a card game I’m good at, lmao. It’s a genre I really adore, but I’m kinda trash at it.

Years went by without much other card game experience. We tried the Dragon Ball Z card game (not the one that’s currently running, the one from the 2000s) and the Digimon Card Game adaptation on Playstation, but we barely had a grip on the rules.

Yu-Gi-Oh video games kept me occupied for the next few years. A series with so many adaptations, it might as well be a video game franchise instead of a manga.

Fast forward at least a decade to 2017, and Slay the Spire’s Early Access reignited my interest in the concept of card games.

Still, I was uninterested in MTG.

I had continued to hear people sings its praises for years, and it made me even more stubborn to not want to try it. Growing up I was always the type of person who had a bad knee-jerk reaction to people heaping praise on stuff. It was constantly lifted up as “the best card game” and “the original trading card game that everything else like Yu-Gi-Oh is a copycat of,” or at least that was the kind of sentiment I was picking up online.

For me, it sounded overhyped. I didn’t like the art, and I didn’t like the sound of how complex it was. It was a period where I wasn’t really interested in Western fantasy stuff, I was much more invested in Japanese properties. I was tired of hearing about card prices and meta decks. I was suspicious of the expensive buy-in and the gambling nature of the booster packs (okay, well, I’m still suspicious of those) and didn’t want to get roped into something like that. I had only one experience in my life where people in the same room as me were even playing MTG or showing their cards to each other, and I stubbornly refused to even lay an eye on it. I sat in the corner and played video games or something.

Meanwhile, my fascination with card game mechanics was growing. I tried other roguelike deckbuilders. I finally played Pokemon again via the Pokemon Trading Card Game Online app. I tried Hearthstone, Shadowverse, Legends of Runeterra, and Marvel Snap.

Eventually I even tried a couple of physical ones, like the new Digimon Card Game and Disney’s Lorcana, with only my partner to try playing it with. There was some sort of hunger in me that would not be satisfied, I loved comparing how the mechanics of the different games worked.

But I needed to find people to play with. And my social anxiety of going to a game shop to play was intensified by the pandemic. I didn’t feel safe sitting shoulder to shoulder with strangers, breathing the same air. While now, in 2026, I’m less worried about getting sick, I still have not tried playing anything at a game shop.

At some point during this process I got an old iPad. I think I was planning to use it to draw (something else that never came to pass) and eventually just used it to play games. Desperate for card games, I finally relented and trying out Magic the Gathering Arena. After a few tutorials and matches, I still wasn’t sure if I liked it, but I took this opportunity to learn how the game worked.

Around this time, I was hearing more about Magic on social media and blog posts and stuff like that. While the dopamine-rush booster pack video type stuff increasingly annoyed me, I began to hear that there was another side of the game. There were people who went to events (or hang-outs with friends) where they just dumped a bunch of cards out and made decks out of what they could. Or they bought decks and boosters and then played using whatever they got, there in the moment. It wasn’t about being the richest or luckiest person who had a cool deck, it was about improvising with the cards you were given (which sounded, to me, similar to a roguelike deckbuilder.)

I would learn, later, that this is the difference between Constructed and Limited play. The variety of formats and types of play in Magic chipped away at my stubbornness of thinking Magic was dumb. It was exactly what I wanted: different options for how to approach the game. If it was a video game, it’d be having different “game modes” to try. One of the few other games I had played that had actually done this, Hearthstone, I thought was better for it.

Card games shouldn’t funnel every player into the exact same standard matchmaking over and over. They should let you use the cards in different, interesting ways. They should be versatile.

Fast forward again, to 2025.

Magic the Gathering releases its Final Fantasy crossover set.

I’m already aware by this point how controversial the crossover sets are in the MTG community. Hasbro is clearly turning the game into some sort of Fortnite-esque IP playground. Compared to Fortnite, the crossovers are often less aesthetically appropriate but at least mechanically interesting.

As an MTG casual, I don’t feel like it’s my place to voice a strong opinion on the Universes Beyond crossovers. I can’t deny that it’s the game trending in a really bad direction, and makes the game almost embarrassing to play. But I guess my stance is this: I think a few of them have been good fits in terms of appropriate theming (Dungeons & Dragons, Lord of the Rings, and Final Fantasy) and a lot of the others have been stupid.

So, with all that said…the Final Fantasy crossover got me to try the game again. And this time, they really got me.

Not in terms of money, mind you. I spent like roughly 10 bucks or something on MTG:A for the Final Fantasy bundle and nothing else. Completely fair trade for how much joy I’ve gotten out of it. But they definitely “got me” in terms of hooking me into the game. I was now making decks that I was really happy with, starring characters that I recognized.

Next, came Lorwyn Eclipsed.

I think sometimes when you’re a fan of something, it’s difficult to gauge how much the thing’s marketing is successful at bringing new fans in. So for the record, I want to throw my anecdotal data point into the mix.

The Final Fantasy crossover got me enthusiastic about Magic and got me to spend 10 bucks. In a sense, it got me into the game.

Then, this Jim Henson Company Lorwyn Eclipsed promo video successfully got me interested in the non-crossover sets and legitimately got me into Lorwyn Eclipsed and got me to spend another 10 bucks or so.

And now, as I’m writing this, the narrative podcasts (audiobooks? whatever) that they’re releasing about the upcoming set Strixhaven have me excited to get invested in that set too.

So whatever you think about all the marketing and the crossovers, rest assured that they do actually work. I’m sure people get into properties like this through dozens of different ways, but it’s not always by having it passed down from some sagely nerd friend. Sometimes it’s just that a company’s marketing actually works, lol.

The appeal of these three things (the Final Fantasy crossover, the Lorwyn music video, the Strixhaven stories) is that it helps break down this really overwhelming franchise into something simple and digestible for me.

Magic the Gathering is really vast and complicated, both because of its gameplay and its lore, so I really need to find a way to take small bites out of it to make my way into it. I know some people get excited by seeing a wall of wiki pages and then jumping in, or are excited by the challenge of a game that’s as complex and crunchy as possible, but that’s not me. I need it boiled down to something approachable first, so I can slowly find my route into it. Like a scared baby deer.

And now, here I am. I’m pretty sure I’ve played a match of Arena almost every day for the past few weeks. I have fun making decks. I have fun imagining how characters I know would play in Magic if they got crossover cards. And most of all I’m just having fun playing the game, even when I lose.

For whatever it’s worth, I don’t regret stubbornly refusing to try Magic for so many years. I think it just wasn’t the right time, I wasn’t in the right headspace to want to get into it. You need the right person or the right spark to kind of shepherd you into things sometimes. And, as I always try to remind myself, the cool thing about missing out on something as a kid is that now you have a cool new thing you can discover as an adult.

I don’t know if I’ll still be playing Magic a year from now, but even if it’s just a phase…I’m glad I finally opened my heart to what it was offering. And sadly, now, I might have to admit after all the hype: it probably is the best trading card game.
